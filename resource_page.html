Intersectionality Implicit Bias

  • Intersectionality

    By way of introduction, what follows are a set of definitions revolving around the concept of intersectionality.

    According to the Oxford English Dictionary, intersectionality is "the interconnected nature of social categorizations such as race, class, and gender, regarded as creating overlapping and interdependent systems of discrimination or disadvantage; a theoretical approach based on such a premise."

    Patricia Hill Collins and Sirma Bilge define Intersectionality as “a way of understanding and analyzing the complexity in the world, in people, and in human experiences. The events and conditions of social and political life and the self can seldom be understood as shaped by one factor. They are generally shaped by many factors in diverse and mutually influencing ways. When it comes to social inequality, people’s lives and the organization of power in a given society are better understood as being shaped not by a single axis of social division, be it race or gender or class, but by many axes that work together and influence each other. Intersectionality as an analytic tool gives people better access to the complexity of the world and of themselves.”

    Ben Shapiro defines intersectionality as “a form of identity politics in which the value of your opinion depends on how many victim groups you belong to. At the bottom of the totem pole is the person everybody loves to hate—the straight, white male.”

  • Implicit Bias

    Implicit bias is an attitude that unconsciously affects our perceptions, actions, and decisions. It often leads to the unequal treatment of people based on race, ethnicity, or gender identity. This concept impacts diverse fields, from medicine to library science, and the effect is generally negative. For instance, a hiring manager might have an unconscious preference for names associated with certain races. This idea was introduced by Mahzarin Banaji and Anthony Greenwald in their 1995 article, "Implicit Social Cognition: Attitudes, Self-Esteem, and Stereotypes."

    A notable tool to measure this is the Implicit Association Test (IAT), provided by a Harvard University project. The authors mentioned above are among its creators, and the project has offered these tests for over 30 years. They provide various thematic tests, allowing participants to choose specific topics. The test is intuitive; users simply make quick, instinctive choices in response to a series of prompts.

    Additionally, the University of Wisconsin-Madison’s WISELI (Women in Science and Engineering Leadership Institute) provides a similar tool called the "Gender & Leadership Implicit Association Test."
